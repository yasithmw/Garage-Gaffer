# Garage Gaffer — Brand Context & Tone of Voice
> Reference this file whenever writing copy, building new pages, or designing UI for Garage Gaffer. It is the single source of truth for how the brand thinks, talks, and presents itself.

---

## 1. What Garage Gaffer Is

**Garage Gaffer** is Bristol's mechanic marketplace — a platform that connects local drivers with vetted, local mobile mechanics who come to you.

The product replaces the traditional garage visit: a driver posts their car problem, gets instant transparent quotes from nearby vetted mechanics, picks one, and the mechanic turns up at their home, office, or kerbside.

**Platform type:** Web-first marketplace (mobile in progress)  
**Geography:** Bristol and surrounding areas — the city, its suburbs, and nearby towns. Not a national platform; the Bristol identity is core to the brand.  
**Stage:** Early product, building trust and community in one city before expanding

### The core problem it solves
The traditional car repair experience is broken for most drivers: opaque pricing, jargon-heavy communication, inconvenient drop-offs, no-shows, and a general sense of being taken advantage of. Garage Gaffer strips all of that out and replaces it with: real people, real prices, real convenience.

## 2. Target Audience

### Primary: The Non-Technical Bristol Driver
- **Age:** 30–50
- **Location:** Bristol (Clifton, Redland, Southville, Bedminster, Stoke Bishop and similar residential postcodes)
- **Relationship with cars:** They drive one, they need it to work, they don't want to become an expert
- **Pain points:**
  - Doesn't know if they're being overcharged
  - Hates the inconvenience of dropping the car off
  - Doesn't understand the jargon mechanics use
  - Doesn't have a trusted mechanic they can rely on
  - Worries about being upsold unnecessary work
- **What they want:**
  - Someone honest and competent who won't take the mick
  - A fair price they can see upfront
  - Minimum disruption to their day
  - To feel looked after, not patronised

### Secondary: Local Bristol Mechanics
- Independent, skilled mechanics looking for reliable work
- Frustrated with running their own admin, chasing payments, finding customers
- Want to be judged on the quality of their work, not their marketing budget

## 3. Brand Personality

Garage Gaffer's personality sits at the intersection of two archetypes:

**The Trusted Local** — warm, community-rooted, real. Like a knowledgeable mate who happens to be a mechanic. Talks like a person, not a company. Knows Bristol. Knows its roads. Has no interest in corporate speak.

**The Reliable Expert** — competent, confident, reassuring. Not flashy, but quietly sure of itself. The brand has done the hard work (vetting, checking, warranty) so the driver doesn't have to think about it.

Together, this creates a brand that feels: **approachable but credible. Local but professional. Plain-spoken but not dumb.**

### What the brand is NOT
- Not a cold, national platform (no "valued customer" energy)
- Not a startup trying to sound disruptive (no "revolutionising" or "reimagining")
- Not a garage (no grease-stained mechanic stereotypes, no macho energy)
- Not patronising (doesn't talk down to drivers who don't know cars)

## 4. Tone of Voice

### The four qualities

**1. Plain and direct**  
Say what you mean. Short sentences. Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate ones. "Find out" not "ascertain". "We check" not "we conduct verification". If a word could be shorter, make it shorter.

**2. Warm but not gushing**  
The warmth comes through in *how* you say things — conversational rhythm, a bit of wit, occasional colloquial British English — not through over-the-top reassurance or filler like "we're passionate about" or "we truly believe."

**3. Confident without bragging**  
State things as facts, not claims. "Every mechanic is DBS checked" lands better than "We're proud to say all our mechanics are rigorously vetted." Let the facts do the work.

**4. Local and specific**  
Mention Bristol. Use it. Name postcodes where relevant. "Your Bristol mechanic" beats "a local mechanic near you." Specificity builds trust.

## 5. Writing Style Rules

### Do this
- Write in second person ("you", "your") — the user is always the main character
- Use active voice: "We vet every mechanic" not "Every mechanic is vetted by us"
- Start sentences with action: "Get a quote. Book. Done."
- Use British English throughout: colour, neighbours, MOT, kerb, authorised
- Use numerals for numbers in UI contexts: "3 quotes", "127 mechanics", "4.87★"
- Use contractions naturally: "We don't", "You won't", "It's"
- Headlines can be fragments: "Sorted." / "Vetted, not just listed." / "No surprises."
- Short paragraphs: 2–3 sentences max in UI contexts
- Use em dashes (—) for rhythm and parenthetical asides
- Use the word "mechanic" — not "technician", "engineer", or "professional"

### Don't do this
- ❌ Jargon: no "torque", "diagnostics protocol", "OBD II" in customer-facing copy
- ❌ Corporate waffle: "leveraging", "ecosystem", "seamless experience", "frictionless"
- ❌ Startup superlatives: "revolutionary", "game-changing", "world-class", "innovative"
- ❌ False urgency: "Don't miss out!", "Act now!" 
- ❌ Passive aggression: "Please note that…", "We ask that you…"
- ❌ Over-reassurance: "Rest assured", "We're here for you", "We truly care"
- ❌ Americanisms: "neighborhood" (use "neighbourhood"), "organize" (use "organise"), "center" (use "centre")
- ❌ Exclamation marks in serious contexts (never in error messages or booking confirmations)
- ❌ Calling the user "user" — they are a "driver" or "you"

## 6. Messaging Framework

### Brand tagline territory
`Your Bristol mechanic, sorted.`

This captures everything in four words: local (Bristol), personal (your), credibility (mechanic), effortless (sorted). Copy should feel like it lives in the same universe as this line.

### Three messaging pillars

**Trust** — "Vetted, not just listed."  
Every mechanic is background-checked, qualification-verified, insured, and rated. This isn't a directory. The brand takes responsibility for who is on the platform.

> Use in: hero sections, mechanic profile pages, FAQs about safety, onboarding trust signals

**Transparency** — "Transparent pricing, zero surprises."  
You see the full price before you commit. Parts, labour, VAT, everything. If anything changes mid-job, the mechanic asks first.

> Use in: pricing pages, quote confirmation flows, FAQs, objection-handling copy

**Convenience** — "At your door, on your clock."  
No waiting rooms. No drop-offs. The mechanic comes to you — at home, at the office, wherever you're parked. Your life doesn't stop for the garage.

> Use in: how-it-works sections, feature highlights, SMS/email notifications

### Supporting proof points (use as trust signals to back up claims)
- Instant quotes — no waiting, no phone calls
- No hidden fees — what you're quoted is what you pay
- 12-month workmanship warranty on all jobs
- DBS-checked and qualification-verified mechanics
- Free to post — no card needed to get quotes
- Same-day slots available

## 7. Competitor Context

### ClickMechanic (primary competitor)
National platform. Feels corporate and slightly impersonal. Known for being convenient but reviews often mention inconsistent mechanic quality and a detached customer service experience. 

**Differentiation angle:** Garage Gaffer is local and accountable. Every mechanic is vetted in person, not just onboarded via a form. The platform knows its city. ClickMechanic is everywhere — Garage Gaffer is *here*.

> Copy tip: Never name competitors directly. Instead, contrast behaviours: "Not a national directory — every mechanic is vetted in person" lands without mentioning names.

### Traditional garages
Convenient for some, but: you have to drop the car off, you're at their schedule, pricing is often opaque, and relationships are hard to build unless you're a regular.

**Differentiation angle:** Garage Gaffer brings the mechanic to you. No drop-offs, no waiting rooms.

### General positioning line to reference
> "We're mechanics and neighbours first, a platform second."

## 8. UI Microcopy Guidance

### Buttons and CTAs
- Primary CTA: `Get quotes` (not "Get a quote" — plural implies choice and speed)
- Secondary: `Browse mechanics` / `How it works` / `Learn more`
- Avoid: "Submit", "Proceed", "Continue" — always use action-describing verbs

### Form labels and placeholders
- Labels: Short and plain — `What's up with it?`, `Car reg`, `Postcode`
- Placeholders: Real-looking examples — `BS8 1TH`, `AB12 CDE`
- Helper text: Sentence case, no full stop — `We use this to find mechanics near you`

### Empty states
- Friendly and specific, never generic: "No quotes yet — mechanics are usually quick, check back in a few minutes."
- Avoid: "No data found", "Nothing here"

### Error messages
- Explain what happened and what to do: `That postcode doesn't look right — try something like BS8 1TH`
- Never blame the user: not "Invalid input" — say what's wrong
- No exclamation marks in errors

### Loading / progress states
- Keep it light: `Finding mechanics near you…`, `Getting your quotes…`
- Never: "Please wait", "Loading…", "Processing request"

### Confirmation copy
- Warm and specific: `You're booked in with James for Tuesday at 9am.`
- Add a reassurance where it earns its place: `He'll confirm the night before.`

### Notifications / push
- Conversational, first-name-first where possible: `James has accepted your quote. He's got everything he needs.`
- Avoid formal notifications: `Your booking has been confirmed by the service provider.`

## 9. Page-Level Guidance for Claude Code

> **Note for layout/content agents:** The optimal hierarchy, section order, and layout structure for each page is to be researched and determined separately. Use the context below to understand the *purpose and user mindset* behind each page type — not as a fixed layout prescription.

### What each page needs to achieve

**Landing page**  
First contact with a sceptical Bristol driver who may have been burned before. The page must quickly answer: "Can I trust this?" and "Is this easy?" — before asking anything of them. Conversion goal: get a quote posted. The brand voice here should be at its most confident and specific.

**How It Works**  
Exists to remove anxiety about an unfamiliar process. The driver shouldn't need to think hard. The flow should feel obvious and short. Reassuring tone — focus on ease, not features.

**Mechanic profiles**  
Trust-building at the individual level. The driver is choosing a specific person to hand their car to. The page needs to make that person feel real, qualified, and accountable — not like a listing on a directory.

**Booking / confirmation flows**  
Post-action states where the driver needs calm reassurance, not excitement. Clear next steps, specific details (who, when, what), and a sense that everything is handled.

**FAQs / Help**  
Objection-handling territory. Drivers here have a specific doubt — answer it directly and quickly. Common concerns: vetting process, pricing guarantees, what happens if something goes wrong, coverage area.

**Error / empty states**  
Keep the driver on-side. Light and practical, not apologetic or robotic.
