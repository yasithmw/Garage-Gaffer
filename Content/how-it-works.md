### Section 2 — The Three Steps

**Component type:** Large numbered step layout — vertical on mobile, horizontal on desktop (or a hybrid: large step numbers anchored left with copy to the right of each, creating a clear vertical flow). Each step: oversized step number (decorative, in brand colour), short label, one–two sentence description. Add a visual connector between steps (line or subtle arrow) to reinforce the linear flow.

**Layout option for desktop:** Three full-width rows — each row contains the step number (large, left), a short label + expanded description (centre), and a visual hint or icon (right). This avoids the cramped three-column approach that loses legibility on medium screens.

**Visual notes:** Step numbers should be very large — a typographic statement, not a bullet point. Icons (if used) should be simple line-style: a speech bubble for Step 1, multiple quotes/envelopes for Step 2, a map pin or door for Step 3. Do not use photographs inside the steps — it breaks the clarity of a process layout.

## Implementation Notes

### For developers

- The `Get quotes` CTA should route to the job-posting flow consistently across all instances on this page — do not vary the destination or the button label.
- The FAQ accordion items should be marked up with FAQPage structured data (schema.org) for SEO benefit — this page is likely to rank for "how does [platform] work" and related queries.
- The "After You Post" timeline (Section 3) can be enhanced in a later iteration with real-time data (e.g. "Average first quote: 47 minutes") — architect the component to accept dynamic values from the API, even if static copy ships first.
- The social proof aggregate stat ("4.9★") should only display once real review volume supports it — build in a minimum threshold check rather than hard-coding the stat.
- Review cards in Section 5: use verified completed job data only. The illustrative samples above are for layout and copy reference only — do not ship placeholder reviews.

### For designers

- Section 2 (The Three Steps) is the primary content block of the page — give it the most breathing room. The step numbers should be a typographic feature, not a badge.
- The "After You Post" timeline (Section 3) needs careful mobile treatment — a horizontal timeline becomes unreadable on small screens. Default to a vertical stacked layout on mobile with clear timestamp labels above each beat.
- The Trust Layer (Section 4) should share the same icon set and visual language as the equivalent section on the Repair, MOT, and Servicing pages — consistent across the site.
- Section 5 review cards: keep the layout compact and scannable. Three short reviews in a row lands faster than two long ones. Truncate quote text at ~120 characters with a "read more" if needed.
- The hero (Section 1) should feel like an arrival, not an apology — don't over-explain in the hero. The process explanation is what the rest of the page is for.

### Content assumptions flagged for review

1. **Aggregate star rating** in Section 5 ("4.9★") is a placeholder — replace with live data before launch.
2. **Quote turnaround time** in Section 2 ("within the hour") and Section 6 FAQ 2 ("within the hour") is an assumption based on competitive norms — confirm against real platform data once jobs are flowing.

## Platform Context Summary

Garage Gaffer is Bristol's mechanic marketplace — a platform that connects local drivers with vetted, local mobile mechanics who come to you. The primary conversion goal is getting a driver to post a job. The brand personality sits between **The Trusted Local** and **The Reliable Expert**: plain-spoken, warm, confident, Bristol-specific. Tone is direct and conversational — no corporate waffle, no jargon.

The **How It Works** page has one job: **remove anxiety about an unfamiliar process.** The driver shouldn't need to think hard. Every line of copy and every layout decision must serve that goal — ease, not features.

Target audience: Non-technical Bristol driver, 30–50, who needs their car fixed but finds the mechanic industry opaque, inconvenient, and intimidating. They are not a car person. They just want it sorted.

## Research Insights

Analysed how comparable platforms structure their How It Works pages — ClickMechanic, Rated People, Checkatrade, Bark.com, and TaskRabbit. Key findings:

**Common patterns across the category:**
- Almost every platform uses a 3-step numbered flow (post → receive quotes → book). The pattern is now a strong mental model for this service type — drivers recognise it instantly.
- Trust signals are typically layered into or directly after the steps — not saved entirely for a separate page.
- Most platforms include a FAQ section lower on the page to mop up remaining anxiety before the CTA.
- Review/testimonial strips appear just before the final CTA on nearly every competitor.

**What stands out (differentiators worth stealing):**
- The best performers make the steps feel fast — copy emphasises how little time it takes ("takes 2 minutes", "most jobs quoted within the hour"). Speed is as reassuring as safety for this audience.
- ClickMechanic leads with the mechanic-comes-to-you convenience angle prominently — this is a strong conversion driver that Garage Gaffer should own even more explicitly, leaning into the Bristol-local angle.
- Rated People and Bark.com bury trust signals too far down. Garage Gaffer should thread trust into the step descriptions themselves, not save it for a separate block.

**UX best practices for How It Works pages:**
- Keep the step count at 3. 4+ steps feel like work. 2 feels incomplete.
- Anchor the page with a hero that states the value before explaining the process.
- The page should not assume the driver knows what "posting a job" means — the language needs to do the translating.
- End with a clear, single CTA. Never split the closing CTA with a secondary ask.
- FAQ on this page should be short (5–6 questions max) and focused on "how does this actually work?" objections — not policy questions that belong in a help centre.
